## The Math That Hurts

Let's do a real job. Not a theoretical one from some consultant's slide deck. One that actually happens.

You're pulling a 750-kilometre run — Albury to Melbourne, one way. Equipment: a rigid truck with a side tippers, burning about 38 litres per 100 kilometres. Round trip is 1,500 kilometres. That's 570 litres of diesel.

At $2.25/L (the NSW average today): **$1,285.50** in fuel.

Now let's say your spreadsheet has $2.10/L in it — from two weeks ago, when prices were lower. Your quote calculation uses that number. Your fuel cost comes out to **$1,197**. The difference between what you quoted and what diesel actually cost you: **$88.50**. You just ate that out of your margin. Maybe out of your profit.

Do that three times in a month and you've lost a grand. On a job you thought you had the numbers for.

The ABC reported in May that a single Hume Highway run burns through **$2,553 in fuel**. At those numbers, a two-cent pricing error isn't pocket change — it's $50 on a run like that. $250 on a week of runs.

## Why Spreadsheets Don't Cut It Anymore

A spreadsheet is a snapshot. A frozen moment from sometime in the past. And diesel prices don't care about your snapshot. They move every time the terminal gate price changes, which in 2026 has been almost every week. The CTAA Alliance tracked diesel TGP rising over 87% since the start of the year. That's not a blip. That's the market doing a thing your spreadsheet can't see.

The guys in the industry who are still going by last week's spreadsheet number aren't wrong to be cautious. Cash flow is tight. Margins are thin. But the people who are checking live prices before every quote? They're the ones who aren't eating losses they don't know about.

Truck fuel costs aren't static. They're dynamic, volatile, and completely out of your control. The only thing in your control is whether you're pricing jobs based on today's numbers or yesterday's.

## How Much Does Diesel Cost on a Trucking Job? It Depends on Three Things

If you're trying to work out what diesel is going to eat into your bottom line on a specific job, it comes down to:

1. **Distance** — obviously. But also route. A flat highway run burns differently than a job with a lot of stop-start, low-range work.
2. **Equipment** — a dozer on a site chewing through 45 litres an hour is a totally different calculation to a rigid doing 20L/100km down the Hume.
3. **The actual price at the pump today** — not the average, not last month's figure, not what your mate reckons. The live number.

Most operators can nail down the first two. They know their rigs. They know their routes. What they can't do — what no spreadsheet can do — is tell them what diesel costs *today* across every station in the country, updated while they're sitting in the cab.
